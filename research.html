Graduate Research (2018-2024)

Advisor: Kristin Eckel-Mahan at the University of Texas Health Science Center in Houston

Telling time from the PVN clock: Chronobiology traditionally views the light-entrained suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) as the "master clock", dictating body-wide rhythms while the remaining body clocks act as "slave oscillators". My dissertation paints a less SCN centered clock hierarchy by focusing instead on the nearby hypothalamic oscillator and metabolic hub, the paraventricular nucleus (PVN).

  • I demonstrated genetic knockout of PVN BMAL1, the protein lynchpin of the PVN clock, dramtically desynchronizes daily metabolic, feeding and activity rhythms in the BMAL1-PVN KO mice culminating in obesity.
  • Using powerful sequencing techniques like single nucleus RNA sequencing, I captured BMAL1’s chromatin and transcriptional dynamics across time and PVN cell types.
  • Transcriptomics highlighted Oxytocin (OXT) as a major oscillating PVN transcript whose rhtyhm I found was blunted in BMAL1-PVN KO mice.
  • Remarkably, daily OXT injections to mimic OXT peaking in the BMAL1-PVN KO partially restored activity rhythms and reduced food intake, revealing a novel role for OXT in circadian activity.

Signficance: My work supports a model whereby the PVN integrates light input from the SCN and metabolic signals from hypothalmic nuclei to fine-tune behavioral and physiological rhythms. Rather than serving as a subordinate “slave oscillator,” the PVN emerges as a key node aligning internal regulation of feeding and activity rhythms with the external day/night cycles.
